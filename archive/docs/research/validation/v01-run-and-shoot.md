## 1. Core Formation and Personnel

### What the archetype document claims:
> "Four-wide, one-back formation."

### Validation: ACCURATE

The Run & Shoot is a 4-WR, 1-RB offense. The personnel positions from left to right are labeled:

- **X** — outside receiver (usually the best route-runner, isolated backside)
- **W** (Wing) — slot receiver, trips side
- **Y** — slot receiver, trips side (runs the critical "seam read")
- **Z** — outside receiver
- **S-Back (Superback)** — the lone running back, a hybrid RB/FB/TE type

The two slot receivers line up one step behind the line of scrimmage (making them "uncovered" and eligible). The offense operates primarily from shotgun, with heavy use of pre-snap motion to diagnose coverage.

There is **no tight end and no fullback** in the pure R&S. This is a defining trait -- the Sentinels' roster correctly reflects this with 3 WRs and no TE/FB in the 7v7 format.

### Modern R&S look:
Nick Rolovich's Washington State (2020-2021) ran a modernized version that kept the 4-wide base but occasionally mixed in tight end personnel or RPO elements. June Jones also evolved the system at Hawaii, adding more run-game wrinkles while keeping the core 4-wide identity.

### What this means for the Sentinels:
The roster is correctly constructed. The note about the 4th WR being "conceptually the RB/motion player" is accurate -- in a full 11-man R&S, you'd have 4 WRs plus the Superback. The 7v7 scaling is reasonable.

## 2. The Core Passing Concepts

The Run & Shoot has four foundational passing concepts, often called the "Fearsome Foursome":

### A. GO (Trips concept)

**Formation:** Trips (3 receivers to one side, X isolated backside).

**Route assignments:**
- Outside trips WR: mandatory go/streak route up the sideline (must release outside)
- Middle slot (Y): runs the **seam read** -- the key route in the entire offense
- Inside slot: quick flat or "sweep" route, 5 yards into the flat, comes off the seam reader's hip (creates a natural rub vs man)
- X (backside): runs a clear-out or secondary read

**The seam read decision tree:**
1. Y reads the near safety post-snap
2. If safety stays deep/middle: Y sits in the void underneath at 12-15 yards (hook/dig)
3. If safety jumps the flat route: Y continues up the seam
4. If safety plays the seam: QB throws the flat

**QB read:** Near safety first. If safety plays up, read off him -- flat or seam depending on his commitment. If safety stays deep, throw the flat.

### B. CHOICE (Doubles concept)

**Formation:** Doubles (2 receivers each side). The primary target is the **isolated X receiver** on the backside.

**Route assignments:**
- X receiver: runs the **choice route** -- the most famous R&S route
- Trips side: runs complementary routes (go, seam read, flat)

**The choice route decision tree (X receiver):**
1. Release off the line, get to 10-yard "decision point"
2. Read the cornerback's leverage and safety positioning:
   - **Corner plays off, no safety help inside:** Break inside on a dig/in route at 10-12 yards
   - **Corner plays press, safety inside:** Release outside, run a fade/go
   - **Corner plays off, safety over the top:** Break on a comeback/out at 10-12 yards
   - **Corner plays soft underneath:** Sit down in the void (hitch)

The choice route is why R&S teams put their **best receiver at X**. The Sentinels correctly have Jaylen "Jet" Monroe (84 OVR, SPEED_LINES badge) described as an "option route specialist -- reads DB leverage and breaks opposite."

### C. SWITCH (Doubles or Trips concept)

**Formation:** Can be run from doubles or trips.

**Route assignments:**
- Two receivers physically cross/swap paths as they release
- Outside WR stems inside; inside slot stems outside
- The crossing action creates a **natural pick/rub** against man coverage

**Decision tree:**
- After the switch, receivers read coverage just like other R&S routes
- The outside receiver (now running inside) reads the seam like a seam read
- The inside receiver (now running outside) takes the vertical/comeback

**Why it matters:** Switch is devastating against man coverage because defenders must communicate and pass off assignments through traffic. It creates confusion and free releases. This concept has transcended the R&S and is now used across all levels of football.

### D. STREAK (Four Verticals)

**Formation:** Doubles or trips.

**Route assignments:**
- All four receivers release vertically
- Outside WRs run pure go routes up the sideline
- One slot runs a mandatory vertical, spacing 2 yards outside the hash
- The other slot runs the **seam read** (the key, as always)

**Seam read against Streak:**
1. **Cover 2 (middle open):** Receiver splits the two safeties on a post
2. **Cover 3 / Cover 1 (middle closed):** Receiver stays up the seam, away from the single-high safety
3. **Safeties very deep:** Receiver cuts underneath to find a void

**QB read:** Eyes go to the seam reader first. The four verticals structure forces safeties into 2-on-4 situations and creates guaranteed one-on-ones somewhere.

### Additional concept: LEVELS

Some R&S trees include a fifth concept called **Levels** (or a variation of Go), where receivers settle at different depths (5, 10, 15 yards) to create layered horizontal reads for the QB. This is more of a modern addition than a core Mouse Davis concept.

### Summary: The archetype document's claim of "2-3 possible breaks based on coverage" is ACCURATE. Every core concept features option routes where the WR adjusts mid-route based on what the defense gives them.

## 3. The Run Game

### What the archetype document claims:
> "It is not a pure air-raid volume attack"

### Validation: ACCURATE -- but could be emphasized more

The R&S **does** have a run game, and it is more substantial than people assume:

**Core run concepts:**
- **Draw:** The primary constraint run. Offensive line pass-sets to sell the pass, then the Superback takes the handoff and hits inside. Exploits defensive backs dropping into coverage and DL rushing upfield.
- **Trap:** Quick-hitting inside run. A pulling guard traps an aggressive DL who has been trained to rush the passer. Works because the 4-wide set forces lighter defensive personnel (nickel/dime DBs replacing LBs).
- **Dive/Inside zone:** Simple downhill run. The 4-wide alignment spreads the defense out, creating a 5-on-7 or even 5-on-6 box advantage. Running inside between the guards/tackles becomes viable because there are fewer defenders in the box.
- **QB scramble:** Not a designed play per se, but a built-in feature. When all four receivers are running option routes, the QB has natural scramble lanes if nothing opens.
- **Screens:** Bubble screens to slots, RB screens, and WR screens. Used as constraint plays to punish aggressive pass rush.

**Run/pass ratio:** Under Rolovich at Hawaii, the offense averaged ~28 runs and ~42 passes per game. That is roughly 40% run, 60% pass -- far more balanced than the Air Raid's typical 22% run / 78% pass under Leach.

**Key insight for TORCH:** The Sentinels' play deck should include 2-3 run/screen plays as constraint concepts. These are not the identity of the offense, but they are essential to keeping defenses honest. Without them, defenses can pin their ears back and rush the passer every snap.

## 4. Run & Shoot vs. Air Raid

### What the archetype document claims:
> "Air Raid is a volume system... Run & Shoot is a reactive system -- fewer concepts but each concept has built-in answers to every coverage."

### Validation: ACCURATE -- this is the correct and widely-accepted distinction

| Dimension | Run & Shoot | Air Raid |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Route philosophy** | WR reads coverage mid-route, adjusts break point | WR runs the called route; QB reads defense to find open man |
| **QB responsibility** | Must be on same page as WR (both read the same key) | Progressions are fixed: 1st read, 2nd read, 3rd read |
| **Playbook size** | Small (4-5 core concepts with infinite variations) | Small (4-5 core concepts run with high volume) |
| **Pass volume** | 35-45 passes/game | 45-55 passes/game |
| **Run game** | Real (~40% of plays): draws, traps, inside zone | Minimal (~20% of plays): mostly constraint |
| **Risk profile** | Higher variance per throw (option routes can be boom or bust) | Lower variance per throw (fixed routes, spacing is schemed) |
| **Miscommunication risk** | HIGH -- if QB and WR read different things, disaster | LOW -- routes are predetermined |
| **Who adjusts?** | The receiver | The quarterback |
| **Ball comes out** | 2.5-3.5 seconds (routes need time to develop reads) | Under 2.5 seconds (timing routes) |

The archetype document's framing is correct: Air Raid is "read the defense" (QB reads); R&S is "react to the defense" (WR reacts). The Serpents (Air Raid) and Sentinels (R&S) are correctly differentiated.

## 5. Historical Programs

### Tiger Ellison (1950s-60s)
- Middletown, Ohio high school coach who conceived the original run-and-shoot concepts
- Published *Run and Shoot Football: Offense of the Future* in 1965
- The philosophical father of the system

### Mouse Davis (1970s-80s)
- Refined and popularized the system at Portland State University
- His Portland State teams threw for enormous yardage in Division II/I-AA
- Coined the terminology (Go, Choice, Switch, Streak) and formalized the option route trees
- Co-created the official Run & Shoot certification program (still active as of 2025)

### Andre Ware / Jack Pardee's Houston (1989)
- **The peak of R&S mania.** Andre Ware won the Heisman Trophy running the system
- Houston averaged 624.9 yards per game (still the FBS all-time record) and 53.5 points per game
- Ware set 26 NCAA records in a single season
- Proved the system could work at the highest level of college football
- **What made it unique:** Pure aggression. Houston threw it constantly and used the R&S's option routes to generate massive explosive plays. The talent level (Ware + elite WRs) made the system nearly unstoppable.

### June Jones' Hawaii (1999-2007)
- Jones played in the R&S and coached under Mouse Davis
- Took over a 0-12 Hawaii program in 1999 and went 9-4 immediately (historic turnaround)
- Peak years: 2006-2007 (23-4 combined record, BCS bowl appearance in 2007)
- **What made it unique:** Jones evolved the run game more than previous R&S coaches. Hawaii ran the ball more frequently and used the Superback as a legitimate between-the-tackles runner, not just a blocker. Jones also added more pre-snap motion and formation variety while keeping the core 4 concepts.

### Nick Rolovich's Washington State (2020-2021)
- The most recent Power Five R&S implementation
- Rolovich was a former Hawaii QB under Jones
- **What made it unique:** Rolovich augmented the R&S with modern concepts -- occasional RPO elements, more screen game, and even some Air Raid staples borrowed from the Mike Leach era at WSU. He ran the ball ~28 times per game compared to Leach's ~16.
- Proved the R&S could be modernized without losing its identity

### NFL: Houston Oilers (early 1990s), Detroit Lions, Atlanta Falcons
- The Oilers under Jack Pardee/Kevin Gilbride ran the R&S with Warren Moon
- Multiple playoff appearances but never won a championship
- The system's NFL decline was accelerated by zone blitz schemes and the 1991 Monday Night Football collapse vs Philadelphia
- Detroit used R&S elements with Barry Sanders and Herman Moore
- Atlanta briefly ran it under Jerry Glanville

## 6. What Actually Counters the Run & Shoot?

### What the archetype document claims:
> Sentinels offense WEAK vs Stags defense (blitz kills route development)

### Validation: PARTIALLY ACCURATE -- but the real picture is more nuanced

The archetype lists **swarm blitz** (Stags defense) as the counter, and the Sentinels' own defense (press man) as their mirror matchup. Let's examine both the historical and schematic counters:

### Counter #1: Zone Blitz (THE historic R&S killer)
- Dick LeBeau invented the zone blitz largely as a response to the R&S
- **How it works:** Rush 5 (including a dropping DL who feigns rush then covers), play zone behind it with 6 defenders. The R&S QB sees pressure and expects man behind it (where option routes feast), but instead gets zone with defenders in passing lanes.
- **Why it works:** The R&S option routes are keyed to man defenders. When a WR reads "inside leverage" and breaks outside, he expects the man defender to be wrong-footed. But if it is zone, there is a zone defender sitting in the area the WR breaks toward.
- **TORCH implication:** The Stags' blitz defense is a reasonable analogue. Heavy pressure + confusion = R&S breakdown.

### Counter #2: Aggressive Press-Man Coverage
- **How it works:** Jam receivers at the line, disrupt their release, prevent them from getting into their route stems cleanly
- **Why it works:** Option routes need a clean release and 10 yards of space to make a read. If the WR is still fighting off a jam at the line, the timing of the entire concept breaks down. The QB expects the WR at a landmark at a certain time; if the WR is late, the window closes.
- **Limitation:** This is NOT a guaranteed counter. R&S receivers are specifically coached in release techniques against press. The Switch concept's crossing action is designed to beat man coverage. Elite WRs (like the Sentinels' Monroe) can win releases consistently.
- **TORCH implication:** Press-man as the Sentinels' OWN defense creating a "mirror" matchup is reasonable -- it is a plausible counter but not an automatic one.

### Counter #3: Disguised Coverage / Pattern Matching
- **How it works:** Show one coverage pre-snap, rotate to another post-snap. The R&S QB and WRs are reading the same coverage key -- if that key lies to them, the WR breaks one way and the QB throws another.
- **Why it works:** The R&S's greatest strength (QB-WR on the same page) becomes its greatest weakness when the defense shows something false. Miscommunication = interception.
- **TORCH implication:** The Serpents' pattern-match defense is listed as "neutral" vs the Sentinels. This could arguably be a soft counter, which makes the matchup matrix slightly generous to the Sentinels. But for game balance, "neutral" works.

### Counter #4: Pure Pressure / Sack-based Defense
- **How it works:** Get to the QB before the option routes develop. R&S routes take 2.5-3.5 seconds to develop (longer than Air Raid timing routes). If you get home in under 2.5 seconds, it doesn't matter what the WR reads.
- **Why it works:** No fullback, no tight end = fewer blockers. The Superback often must stay in to block, reducing the offense to 3 receivers.
- **Limitation:** If the R&S has a mobile QB or good hot routes, pressure can be exploited.
- **TORCH implication:** This is the Stags' swarm blitz identity. The archetype document correctly identifies this as the R&S's primary weakness.

### Overall weakness assessment:
The archetype's counter-play matrix is **well-designed**. The Stags' blitz defense as the hard counter is historically accurate (pressure kills R&S). The Sentinels' press-man as the mirror is reasonable. The one area that could be slightly off is the Serpents' pattern-match being listed as "neutral" rather than a soft counter, but for game balance it works.

### The archetype's claim that "press-man jams destroy air raid timing" (Sentinels DEF strong vs Serpents OFF) is also correct and well-sourced. Press coverage specifically disrupts the fixed timing routes that Air Raid depends on.

## 7. Sentinels Play Cards (10 Offensive Plays)

These 10 plays map to real Run & Shoot concepts and constraint plays, reflecting the Sentinels' identity: mostly passing (7 pass plays), with constraint runs and screens (3 plays). Mix of risk levels across the deck.

```
Name: Choice Route
Category: SHORT PASS
Risk: MED
Flavor: "Read him and burn him"
```
*Maps to: The R&S Choice concept. X receiver reads corner leverage at 10 yards and breaks opposite. The signature R&S play -- intermediate, high-percentage when the read is correct.*

---

### Play 2
```
Name: Go Seam
Category: DEEP PASS
Risk: HIGH
Flavor: "Split the safeties deep"
```
*Maps to: The R&S Go concept. Trips formation, slot receiver runs the seam read against the safety. Explosive when the safety bites on the flat route. High risk because it requires time to develop and a correct read.*

---

### Play 3
```
Name: Switch
Category: SHORT PASS
Risk: LOW
Flavor: "Cross 'em up underneath"
```
*Maps to: The R&S Switch concept. Two receivers cross paths, creating a natural pick against man coverage. Short, high-percentage throw after the rub creates separation.*

---

### Play 4
```
Name: Streak
Category: DEEP PASS
Risk: HIGH
Flavor: "Four arrows, one target"
```
*Maps to: The R&S Streak / Four Verticals concept. All four receivers release vertically, stressing safeties into impossible 2-on-4 choices. The seam reader finds the void. Boom-or-bust play.*

---

### Play 5
```
Name: Quick Flat
Category: QUICK PASS
Risk: LOW
Flavor: "Dump it and run"
```
*Maps to: The flat route in the Go concept. Quick throw to the slot running a 5-yard sweep route into the flat. Safe, short, gets the ball to a playmaker in space. The "take what they give you" throw.*

---

### Play 6
```
Name: Seam Read
Category: SHORT PASS
Risk: MED
Flavor: "Find the soft spot"
```
*Maps to: The seam read route that underpins every R&S concept. The slot receiver reads the safety and sits in the void between zones at 12-15 yards. The R&S bread-and-butter -- not flashy, just consistently open.*

---

### Play 7
```
Name: Superback Draw
Category: DRAW
Risk: LOW
Flavor: "Sell the pass, run the ball"
```
*Maps to: The R&S draw play. OL pass-sets, QB fakes a pass read, then hands to the Superback hitting inside. Exploits aggressive pass rush and light boxes (4-wide pulls LBs and safeties out). The core constraint run.*

---

### Play 8
```
Name: Fade & Stop
Category: DEEP PASS
Risk: MED
Flavor: "Freeze him, then go"
```
*Maps to: The choice route variation where the X receiver sells a go route, stops at 12 yards if the corner bails, or keeps running if the corner squats. An option route disguised as a deep shot. Medium risk because it depends on the WR-QB connection.*

---

### Play 9
```
Name: Bubble Screen
Category: SCREEN
Risk: LOW
Flavor: "Quick flip to the edge"
```
*Maps to: Modern R&S screen game. Quick lateral pass to the slot WR with blockers fanning out. Constraint play that punishes aggressive corner/safety play and keeps the defense from teeing off on the pass rush. Low-risk, moderate-reward.*

---

### Play 10
```
Name: QB Scramble Drill
Category: QB RUN
Risk: MED
Flavor: "Nothing open? I'll go"
```
*Maps to: The scramble element of the R&S. When four receivers are running option routes downfield, the QB has natural running lanes if the rush arrives before the reads develop. Not a designed run -- it is a "break glass" play that keeps the defense honest against a mobile QB. Medium risk because the QB is exposed.*
