## 1. Core Formation: The Flexbone

### Real-World Personnel (11v11)

The flexbone is derived from the wishbone and features:

- **QB** — Under center. Must be a decisive runner, tough enough to absorb hits on the keep. Not required to be a great passer, but must threaten the throw on play-action to keep safeties honest.
- **B-Back (Fullback)** — Lined up directly behind the QB, 4-5 yards deep. The workhorse. Receives the majority of carries on the dive. Must be strong enough to break arm tackles and move the pile. Think: 5'10", 220 lbs, runs a 4.7 but breaks three tackles per carry.
- **Two A-Backs (Slotbacks)** — Aligned just outside the tackles, off the line of scrimmage (roughly where a slot receiver would be, but 1 yard behind the LOS). These are hybrid RB/WR types. They serve as the pitch man on option plays, motion across the formation for rocket toss/counter, block on the perimeter, and become receivers on play-action passes.
- **Two Wide Receivers** — Split wide, primarily as blockers on the perimeter (stalk-blocking corners and safeties). They run routes only on play-action, which makes those routes devastating when they come.
- **Five Offensive Linemen** — Standard, but their blocking rules are different from most offenses. They are NOT trying to block everyone. Specific defenders are left unblocked by design (the "read" defenders). The OL combo-blocks, reaches, and seals gaps rather than man-blocking every defender.

### Personnel Grouping

The flexbone typically uses **30 personnel**: 3 backs (1 FB + 2 SB), 0 tight ends, 2 wide receivers. Some teams occasionally add a tight end ("22 personnel") or go double-flex with both WRs tight to the formation.

### 7v7 Adaptation for TORCH

The archetype document scales this correctly:
- **QB** (Colton Briggs) — option QB, runner who can throw
- **3 OL** (Maddox, Whitfield, Jernigan) — combo/reach blockers
- **FB** (Marcus "The Hammer" Thorne) — star player, dive back
- **2 SB** (Isaiah Quick, Deon Hargrove) — pitch/motion players

## 2. The Triple Option Mesh: How the Read Actually Works

### The Decision Sequence

The triple option is a **two-read, three-option play**. The QB reads two unblocked defenders sequentially:

**Read 1 — The Dive Key (typically the playside defensive end or 5-technique):**
1. QB takes the snap, reverse-pivots, and places the ball in the B-back's belly as the B-back attacks the A-gap or B-gap. This is the **mesh point**.
2. The QB watches the dive key (the first defender outside the playside tackle who is deliberately left unblocked).
3. **If the dive key squeezes down** to take away the fullback dive → QB **pulls** the ball from the mesh and moves to Read 2.
4. **If the dive key stays wide or hesitates** → QB **gives** to the fullback. The FB runs through the designed gap. Play over for the QB (he fakes the keep/pitch to hold the edge defenders).

**Read 2 — The Pitch Key (typically the next defender outside, often the OLB or force player):**
5. If the QB pulled the ball, he now attacks downhill toward the pitch key while the playside A-back trails in pitch relationship (4x1 — 4 yards deep, 1 yard behind).
6. **If the pitch key commits to the QB** → QB **pitches** to the A-back, who turns the corner with blockers ahead.
7. **If the pitch key widens to take the pitch** → QB **keeps** and turns upfield inside the pitch key.

### Why It Works

The genius is mathematical: the offense leaves two defenders unblocked but reads them both. Traditional offenses block every defender, which requires winning at the point of attack. The triple option sidesteps this entirely — you don't need to beat a defender if you can just read him and go where he isn't. This is why smaller, less athletic teams (service academies) can compete with superior talent: the scheme neutralizes the opponent's best defenders by never blocking them.

### The Mesh Point in Detail

The mesh lasts approximately 0.5-0.8 seconds. The QB extends the ball with both hands into the B-back's belly. The B-back clamps his arms over the ball but does NOT squeeze — the QB retains control. The B-back runs his track regardless of the read; he runs as if he has the ball whether he does or not. If the QB gives, the B-back secures and hits the hole. If the QB pulls, the B-back continues his fake (selling the dive to linebackers).

**Validation of archetype doc:** The description "QB reads the DE and pitches in rhythm" is accurate but slightly simplified. The QB reads two defenders sequentially (dive key, then pitch key), not just the DE. The archetype doc's description of "reads defensive linemen rather than blocking them" is exactly correct.

## 3. Key Plays Beyond the Base Triple

### Inside Veer (the "base" triple option)

The standard triple option. The B-back's dive path attacks **inside** — between the guard and tackle (B-gap). The dive key is the playside DE (first down lineman from the tackle out). After the give/pull decision, the pitch key is the next defender outside. This is the bread-and-butter, run 20+ times per game.

### Outside Veer

Same triple-option read sequence, but the B-back's dive path moves **outside the tackle** toward the C-gap. This forces the outside linebacker to be the dive key rather than the DE. Changes the geometry of the entire play and attacks a different part of the defense.

### Midline Option

A two-option (double option) play that reads an **interior** defender — usually the nose tackle or 3-technique DT. The B-back takes the dive through the A-gap, and the QB reads the DT:
- DT takes the dive → QB keeps and runs behind the center
- DT stays put → give to the B-back

There is no pitch phase. This is Army's bread-and-butter under Jeff Monken. It attacks the interior when the defense over-commits to stopping the outside option.

### Midline Triple

An extension of midline that adds a pitch option. After the QB reads the interior DT and keeps, he has a trailing A-back in pitch relationship and reads a second defender (the DE or OLB). Gives three options from an interior read rather than a perimeter read.

### Speed Option

A **two-man option** — just QB and pitch back, no dive fake. The QB takes the snap and immediately attacks the edge at full speed. He reads the pitch key on the run:
- Pitch key takes the QB → pitch outside
- Pitch key widens → QB turns upfield

Navy favors this. It is faster to develop than the full triple and is devastating against defenses that are keying on the fullback dive. Conceptually simple, but requires a fast QB.

### Rocket Toss (Jet Sweep variant)

The QB receives the snap and immediately tosses to a motioning A-back who is already at full speed heading to the perimeter. The A-back receives the ball approximately one yard outside the tackle. No read involved — pure speed to the edge with lead blockers. This stretches the defense horizontally and is particularly effective against:
- Interior-focused defenses that stack the box
- Blitz-heavy schemes (the ball is out before the blitz arrives)

### Counter Option

Uses misdirection. The backfield action flows one direction (fullback dives to the right), but the play attacks the opposite side. The backside A-back comes back across the formation, and the QB either hands off on the counter or runs the option to the weak side. This punishes over-pursuit and aggressively shifting defenses.

### Zone Dive

A simpler, zone-blocked inside run for the B-back. No option read — the OL zone-blocks and the B-back reads the blocks and cuts accordingly. Used as a change-up when the defense is over-keying on the option reads.

**Validation:** The archetype doc's scheme tags (`flexbone`, `triple_option`, `fullback_dive`, `pitch_read`, `midline`, `clock_control`) are all legitimate concepts. The existing Iron Ridge data file already includes triple_option, zone_read, midline, rocket_toss, and qb_keeper — all real plays from this system.

## 4. The Passing Game: Rare but Devastating

Flexbone teams typically pass 15-20% of the time (8-12 passes per game). But when safeties creep into the box to stop the run, the passing game becomes lethal. Every pass play uses play-action off the option fake.

### Play-Action Post-Wheel

The signature flexbone pass play. The QB fakes the triple option mesh (B-back dive fake, A-back motion), then rolls out. Routes:
- **Playside WR** runs a deep **post** (mimicking a crack block on the safety, who bites on the run fake)
- **Backside A-back** runs a **wheel route** — starts flat, then turns upfield along the sideline
- QB reads post first (big play), wheel second (high-percentage throw)

This is the play that produces 40+ yard touchdowns against run-heavy defensive looks.

### Switch Concept

A staple of option-offense passing. Designed to exploit defensive aggressiveness:
- The **WR** fakes a stalk block (as if run play), then releases on a **tube/drag route** underneath
- The **A-back** fakes a perimeter block, then runs a **wheel route** deep outside
- The two routes "switch" vertical levels, creating a high-low read

Against single-high safety (Cover 1/3): the tube route underneath is usually open.
Against two-high safety (Cover 2): the wheel route outside is usually open.

### Bootleg Flat

QB fakes the dive, boots away from the play-action flow. Throws to the A-back on a flat/swing route or the backside WR on a crossing route. Short, high-percentage throw. Used on third-and-medium or when the defense is selling out to stop the run.

### Play-Action Deep Shot ("Titan" concept)

When the defense shows a playside safety, the QB can call a sight-adjust at the line:
- Playside WR converts to a **corner route**
- Playside A-back runs a **wheel-stop**
- Backside WR runs a **deep post**

This is the home-run ball — low frequency, high reward.

### Hitch-Seam

Both WRs run 5-yard hitch routes (quick, safe throws), and the playside A-back runs a seam route up the middle. Simple three-level concept that gives the QB an easy read: seam if the LBs bite on the run fake, hitch if they don't.

### Passes off Rocket Toss Action

The QB fakes the rocket toss (A-back in jet motion), and the defense flows hard to the motion side. The backside is now vacated. Routes attack the backside with posts, digs, and crossing routes into the empty space.

**Validation:** The archetype doc's description of "Passing is rare (15-20% of plays) but devastating when the safeties creep up" is exactly right. The passing game is entirely built off play-action, and it works because defensive players who have been conditioned for 30+ run plays cannot help but bite on the fake.

## 5. Historical Programs

### Paul Johnson — The Godfather of the Modern Flexbone

Paul Johnson ran the flexbone triple option at every stop of his career:
- **Georgia Southern (1997-2001):** Two national championships (I-AA). Proved the system could dominate.
- **Navy (2002-2007):** Led NCAA Division I-A in rushing yards 3 of his last 4 years. Took Navy to relevance with the option. Pioneered formations like the "double-flex" (both WRs tight to the formation).
- **Georgia Tech (2008-2018):** The first BCS/P5 conference school in ~20 years to run the flexbone. Proved it could work against SEC-level athletes. Beat teams like Clemson, Virginia Tech, and Miami with the system. His Georgia Tech teams occasionally led the nation in rushing.

Johnson's version emphasized the inside veer as the base play, with the midline, counter option, and speed option as complements. He was disciplined about play-calling — he would run the same play 15 times if the defense couldn't stop it.

### Army (Jeff Monken, 2014-present)

Monken was a Johnson disciple. Army's version is **more interior-focused** than Navy's:
- Heavy use of the **midline option** (reading the DT, attacking A-gaps)
- The QB and FB run between the tackles more than around the edge
- More of a "power" option identity — downhill, physical, grind-it-out
- In 2024, Army went 11-1 and won the AAC championship. Their QB rushed for 32 TDs and finished 6th in Heisman voting.

### Navy (Ken Niumatalolo, then Brian Newberry)

Navy's version is **more perimeter-oriented**:
- Heavy use of **speed option** and **rocket toss** to get to the edge
- More formation variety — the "double-flex" alignment, unbalanced lines, shifting
- Navy's 2024 season: 11-2, ranked 23rd in the final AP Poll
- Under new offensive coordinator Drew Cronic, Navy began installing a "millennial Wing-T" that marries Wing-T, triple option, and spread concepts

### Air Force

Air Force runs the flexbone with its own wrinkles but has been less consistently dominant than Army/Navy in recent years. Their version features standard triple option concepts with emphasis on the fullback dive and zone-dive variations.

### Georgia Tech Post-Johnson

When Geoff Collins replaced Johnson in 2019, he immediately abandoned the triple option for a spread offense. The transition was disastrous — Georgia Tech won just 10 games over three seasons. This reinforced the conventional wisdom that the triple option requires years of installation and specific recruiting to work, and you cannot simply "switch to it" or "switch away from it" quickly.

### Modern Context

A 2022 NCAA rule banning cut blocks reduced the triple option's effectiveness (cut blocks were a critical tool for undersized OL to handle larger DL). But the service academies adapted. The system survives because:
1. **Roster stability** — Service academy players don't transfer. In an era where 2,600+ players enter the transfer portal annually, the academies have guaranteed continuity.
2. **Scheme leverage** — The option still neutralizes athletic mismatches by never blocking the best defenders.
3. **Preparation time** — Opponents only face the option once a year, meaning they get one week to prepare for a scheme they never see.

**Validation:** The archetype doc's inspiration section ("Army/Navy/Georgia Tech under Paul Johnson") is accurate and covers the right programs. The note about service academies using the system to "compete with superior athletes by using scheme leverage" is exactly the historical rationale.

## 6. What Actually Stops the Triple Option?

### The Archetype Doc Claims:
1. **Swarm blitz** (Stags defense) — "blitz overwhelms the option mesh before reads can develop"
2. **Pattern-match LBs** (Serpents defense) — "LBs read the mesh point and the disguise means the QB cannot pre-read the defense"

### Real-World Defensive Counters:

**Counter 1: Assignment Football / Gap Discipline**

The most fundamental defense against the option. Every defender has a specific assignment:
- **Dive player** — typically the DE or a scraping LB, responsible for tackling the FB
- **QB player** — another defender (LB or safety) responsible for the QB on the keep
- **Pitch player** — the force defender (OLB, SS, or rolled-up corner) responsible for the pitch back

If all three players execute their assignments, the option gains nothing. The challenge: this requires extreme discipline and practice, and one missed assignment creates an explosive play.

**Counter 2: Scrape Exchange**

A specific technique where the DE and LB swap responsibilities on the fly:
- The **DE crashes hard inside** to take the dive (instead of being the read key, he becomes a blocker-eater)
- The **LB scrapes over the top** to take the QB

This confuses the QB's read because the dive key (DE) is crashing, which should cue a "pull" read, but the LB is immediately there to tackle the QB. The QB sees "pull" but runs into the scraping LB. Very effective, but requires fast, athletic LBs who can scrape quickly.

**Counter 3: Slow-Play / Feather Technique**

The pitch key doesn't commit to either the QB or the pitch. Instead, he "feathers" — shuffles laterally, maintaining leverage on both. This forces the QB to hold the ball longer, giving pursuit time to rally. It doesn't produce TFLs, but it limits the option to 2-3 yard gains instead of explosive plays.

**Counter 4: Blitzing / Swarm Pressure**

Sending extra defenders at the mesh point before the reads develop. If the blitz gets to the QB before he can complete the dive read, the entire play breaks down. This is what the Stags defense represents.

**Is it accurate?** Partially. Blitzing CAN work against the option, but it is actually a **risky** counter, not a reliable one. Here's why:
- If the blitz comes from the wrong gap, the option exploits the vacated space
- The rocket toss specifically attacks blitz-heavy defenses (the ball is out before the pressure arrives)
- Counter option punishes over-aggressive pursuit from blitzes
- The best option teams have "check with me" calls that audible to the play that exploits the blitz

In real football, the most reliable counter to the triple option is disciplined assignment football (Counters 1-3), NOT blitzing. However, for TORCH's game balance, having the swarm blitz as the "strong" counter works because:
- It creates a clear rock-paper-scissors dynamic
- The card game simplifies the "preparation time" problem (in real football, the option is hard to stop because you only see it once a year; in TORCH, you play against it regularly)
- The game abstracts the risk of the blitz — in the card game, the blitz either disrupts or it doesn't, without the granular gap-discipline simulation

**Counter 5: Pattern-Match Coverage (Serpents)**

The archetype doc says pattern-matching LBs "read the mesh" and "match the option backs." This is a creative interpretation rather than a literal football concept. In real football:
- Pattern matching is a **pass coverage** technique, not a run defense technique
- LBs in pattern-match schemes are reading receivers' routes, not option mesh points
- Against the run (including option), these LBs would use their normal run-fit rules

However, the underlying logic IS defensively sound: Saban-style defenses that use disguise and pattern matching tend to have the most disciplined, well-coached linebackers in the country. These LBs are trained to read keys, not guess. That discipline transfers to option defense. The "disguise" element also has merit — if the QB can't pre-snap identify where the pitch lane will be, his third read becomes harder.

**Verdict:** The archetype doc's counter-matchups are **defensively reasonable for game balance** even if they simplify the real football mechanics. The swarm blitz as a counter is slightly generous (in reality it's risky), but it creates good gameplay tension. The pattern-match counter is more of a "disciplined, well-coached LBs" counter dressed in pattern-match clothing, which works thematically.

## Comparison to Existing Iron Ridge Plays

The current Iron Ridge team (ground-and-pound) already uses some triple option plays: `triple_option`, `zone_read`, `midline`, `rocket_toss`, `qb_keeper`. The Wolves' playbook should feel distinct from Iron Ridge by:

1. **More option plays** — Iron Ridge has 4 option/QB run plays. The Wolves should have 5 (Inside Veer, Midline, Speed Option, Counter Option, QB Sneak).
2. **No zone read** — Zone read is a spread concept. The Wolves run under-center flexbone, not shotgun spread. Replace zone_read with inside veer.
3. **No power/trap** — Those are man-blocking, downhill run schemes. The Wolves read defenders rather than blocking them. Replace with fullback dive (similar effect but different scheme philosophy).
4. **Play-action instead of generic pass** — Iron Ridge has PA Flat and PA Post. The Wolves should have PA Post-Wheel (specific flexbone concept) and PA Bootleg (specific flexbone concept) plus the Switch Pass.
